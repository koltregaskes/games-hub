# Review Process, Previews, And Ethics

## Pick the right format before you pick the score

### Use a preview when

- the build is early
- large features are still missing
- online or live-service systems cannot be judged properly
- you mostly want to talk about shape and promise

### Use a review in progress when

- the game is substantially playable
- the reader still deserves launch-time guidance
- one important part of the experience is not yet reliable enough to score

### Use a full scored review when

- the experience being sold is actually testable now
- the writer has seen enough of the loop to judge it honestly
- the verdict can stand as a snapshot of the game at that moment

## Match the writer to the game

One of the clearest lessons from big outlets is that pairing matters.

- Give the game to someone who understands the genre.
- Prefer a writer who is genuinely interested in the release.
- Keep editorial calibration so the verdict is not wildly out of step with the written case.

## Disclose the review conditions

Be upfront about:

- platform
- build or patch state
- whether the copy was public, preview, private, or review code
- whether the writer attended a hands-on event
- whether major systems were unavailable

That context is part of the review.

## Do not manipulate feedback loops

Steam’s rules are useful here even for internal studio thinking:

- do not solicit reviews for rewards
- do not try to game the score
- do not argue with honest negative feedback
- do not treat criticism as disloyalty

If the audience keeps calling out the same problem, the problem is probably real enough to investigate.

## Use reviews and previews differently

### Preview output

- strongest promise
- biggest unknown
- why the game matters
- what must be proven next

### Review output

- verdict on what exists now
- score if evidence is sufficient
- clear recommendation
- concrete weaknesses that matter to the current player

## Updated reviews and post-launch changes

GameSpot’s updated-review idea is useful for live products.

If a game changes materially:

- do not quietly rewrite the old review
- keep the old review as a dated snapshot
- publish a new dated verdict

That helps teams see progress honestly instead of pretending the launch state never happened.
