# How To Score A Game

Scores are useful, but only if they behave like a summary of the argument instead of a substitute for it.

The big outlets use different scales:

- [GameSpot](https://www.gamespot.com/review-guidelines/) uses a whole-number ten-point scale.
- [PC Gamer](https://www.pcgamer.com/pc-gamer-reviews-policy/) uses a percentage scale.
- [GamesRadar+](https://www.gamesradar.com/review-guidelines-how-we-score/) uses a five-star scale with half-stars.
- A Eurogamer-style model often leans more on recommendation language than on exact numbers.

For our internal studio reviewer, the cleanest approach is:

## Use one whole-number ten-point scale

No decimals by default.

That keeps the score stable, readable, and easy to compare over time.

### Our internal meaning

- `10` Essential. A landmark demo or release. Rare.
- `9` Superb. Easy to recommend. Distinctive, polished, and memorable.
- `8` Great. Strong enough that most target players should pay attention.
- `7` Good. Clearly worthwhile, but comes with visible caveats.
- `6` Fair. Promising and partially effective, but not comfortably recommendable yet.
- `5` Mixed. Functional, inconsistent, or undercooked.
- `4` Poor. Major problems hold the whole thing back.
- `3` Bad. There is a real game-shaped idea here, but the build is still a struggle.
- `2` Terrible. Broken, joyless, or too incomplete to recommend.
- `1` Abysmal. Borderline unplayable or fundamentally failed as a player experience.

## Score the right thing

### If it is a demo

Score the demo as a demo.

Ask:

- Does it communicate the fantasy?
- Does it let the player do the important thing?
- Does it feel coherent enough to recommend as a demo?

Do not punish a demo for not containing the whole eventual game.
Do punish it if it fails at being a convincing demo.

### If it is unfinished

Prefer a preview or review in progress instead of a forced score.

### If it changed materially after launch

Write a new updated review.

## Use a breakdown before the final number

Score these dimensions first:

1. identity and hook
2. core loop
3. onboarding and usability
4. content density and closure
5. technical performance and stability
6. audiovisual craft
7. recommendation strength

Then ask one final editorial question:

Does the number reflect what the written review actually argues?

If not, change the number.

## Anti-inflation rules

- A promising idea is not automatically a `7`.
- Stylish presentation is not enough to carry a weak loop.
- Do not score on future potential.
- If the review reads like a `5`, do not end on an `8`.

The score should help the team focus, not flatter it.
